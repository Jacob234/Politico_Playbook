TOP TALKER

Former President Joe Biden speaks during the National Bar Association's 100th Annual Awards Gala in Chicago on Thursday, July 31, 2025. | Nam Y. Huh/AP

DIRE WARNING: Former President Joe Biden stopped in Chicago on Thursday to deliver a warning that “dark days” are ahead under the Trump administration.

Democracy eroding: “We are, in my view, at such a moment in American history, reflected in every cruel executive outreach, every rollback of basic freedoms, every erosion of long-standing, established precedent,” Biden told the nearly 1,100 gathered at the National Bar Association's gala dinner at the Hyatt. Read our home page story here

Biden accused the Trump administration of rolling back civil rights, eroding legal norms and endangering the democratic fabric of the nation.

He faulted Congress for its passivity and accused the White House of trying to “erase truth.” He called the administration “cruel” — pointing to legal immigrants detained in handcuffs and attacks on media and law firms. “That’s not hyperbole,” he insisted.

Biden’s address wasn’t just politics — it was part memoir, part warning and part rallying cry. He recalled the 1968 riots, Dr. Martin Luther King Jr.’s assassination, and his early days as a public defender in Delaware. Biden also touted his own record — appointing more Black women to the bench than any president before him, as well as backing historically Black colleges and universities.

Ever the optimist: Biden said the stakes are high, but added, “America is worth fighting for.”

Big names attending the reception or dinner included the Rev. Jesse Jackson, who was honored for his civil rights service, his son, Rep. Jonathan Jackson, Ariel Investment’s John Rogers Jr., Rep. Robin Kelly, Rep. Danny Davis, state Rep. La Shawn Ford, Commissioner Larry Rogers Jr., Chicago Bears President Kevin Warren, Rainbow Push Rev. Janette Wilson and Chicago Mayor Brandon Johnson, who welcomed the crowd.

At a press conference ahead of Biden’s speech, National Bar Association President Wiley Adams said he wanted to hear from Biden how he was navigating his health struggles and if he was doing okay. But the former president, who is being treated for prostate cancer, never did address his health, by the Sun-Times’ Violet Miller.

Applause, applause: The lawyers’ association honored Biden for his work in office, including his appointments of Black U.S. Supreme Court Justice Ketanji Brown Jackson and a diverse slate of federal judges, by the Tribune’s Olivia Olander.
THE BUZZ

Congressman Danny Davis, left, and state Rep. La Shawn Ford share a light moment after Davis endorsed Ford on Thursday, July 31, 2025. | Shia Kapos/POLITICO

IT’s OFFICIAL: Rep. Danny Davis won’t seek reelection, and he’s endorsing state Rep. La Shawn Ford for his 7th District seat.

“I am 83 years old,” Davis told supporters Thursday outside his West Side office. “Some people won’t get to be 83… I just decided that this would be a good time — this would be a great time — to try and usher in new leadership.”

Ford's rallying cry: “This campaign isn’t just about securing your vote,” Ford said Thursday. “It’s about sending a firm message against ‘Make America Hate Again.’”

With Davis out of the race, the list of Democrats wanting to run is expected to grow. Forest Park Mayor Rory Hoskins announced his bid after Davis's news conference. “With deep respect for the people of this district, I am stepping forward to continue this work — not by replicating the past, but by offering bold, proven leadership for the future,” Hoskins said in a statement.

Davis’s other big move: The congressman said he won’t seek reelection for his Democratic State Central Committee seat. He isn’t offering an endorsement in that contest. After Davis’s press conference, Illinois House Speaker Emanuel “Chris” Welch said in a statement that he plans to run for the Democratic committeeman position.

Reader Digest

We asked about gathering signatures for candidates.

John Anderson: “I got bit by a dog while knocking doors to collect signatures when I ran for circuit judge in 2010. Little dogs have sharp teeth. It worked out OK — they put a sign in their yard for me.”  

Matthew Beaudet: “When I ran for judge countywide in 2000, my 76-year-old dad went to senior buildings and got a ton of signatures from widowed female residents. My 76-year-old mom stayed at home and told my dad to behave himself in the senior buildings.”

Brady Chalmers: “I was knocking doors for Obama in Virginia in '07. I knocked on a guy's door and did my spiel. He looks at me and says, ‘I've never voted for a N-word in my life, but I'm gonna give this Obama a chance.’ I had no idea how to react to the situation, but I was pretty sure we were going to win the election at that point.”

Anne Sommerkamp: “A little boy (3 or 4 years old) answered the door naked with an Xbox controller in his hand. His mom was horrified and embarrassed. I told her my son liked being naked at that age, too. It’s a boy thing he’ll outgrow. She signed, and chuckled all day.”
